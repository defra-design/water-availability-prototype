Water trading

If you only need water for a short period of time, instead of applying for a licence which can take a while to process, you may be able to enter into an agreement with an existing licence holder.

You can agree for them to give you part or all of their water abstraction rights permanently or temporarily. This is called the trading of water abstraction rights. Trades need approval by the Environment Agency to protect the environment and the rights of other lawful water users.

View a list of existing abstractors in your area.

Building a reservoir

{% else %}

Building a reservoir

{% endif %}

If local water availability is low over summer and higher over winter, building a reservoir is one option to ensure a more consistent water supply. Read the guide to planning, designing, constructing and commissioning a water storage reservoir for more information.

Building a reservoir will still require a licence, however you will have more flexibility over when to abstract, and so a winter only licence may be an option. In many cases they also require a lower volume per day to abstract compared to other uses (for example spray irrigation), making them a good choice in areas where summer flows are too low for other uses, or where restrictions would prevent year-round abstraction.

Local Resource Option (LRO) is a water resource solution designed to enhance water resilience or supply for a small group of abstractors within their area. Owned, operated, and managed by the abstractors themselves. LROs range from infrastructure projects like reservoirs and rainwater harvesting systems to collaborative solutions such as water sharing agreements or licence pooling.

Water recycling and efficiency

{% else %}

Water recycling and efficiency

{% endif %}

The Environment Agency believes water recycling can play an effective and increasingly important role in securing water supplies. Indirect recycling treats wastewater and discharges it to an environmental buffer, such as a river, reservoir or underground aquifer. Water can then be abstracted and treated further.

Increasing water efficiency is also encouraged when local water availability is low. Smart irrigation scheduling, reducing leakages and reusing water for wash down are all options to save water. Waterwise offer tips and information on water efficiency and conservation.

Rainwater collection and storage

{% else %}

Rainwater collection and storage

{% endif %}

Rainwater harvesting is an efficient way to use water.

Harvested rainwater is rainwater:

  • collected from roofs and other above ground surfaces
  • collected via a system of above ground pipes and tanks
  • isolated from inland waters or groundwater

The Environment Agency will not regulate harvested rainwater provided its use does not harm the environment - either alone or combined with other abstractions or transfers. Rainwater harvesting within a catchment must not affect the normal watercourse flow.

Learn more about rainwater harvesting and regulation in the Environment Agency's Rainwater harvesting: regulatory position statement.

Desalination

{% else %}

Desalination

{% endif %}

Desalination is the treatment of brackish (slightly salty water from river estuaries) or seawater to remove dissolved salts and other organic chemicals to provide a source of fresh water. After treatment, this can be suitable for drinking.

It is a more expensive and complicated option to implement but is being used successfully in other countries and becoming more popular.

For further information, take a look at the Environment Agency position statement on desalination for public water supply.
